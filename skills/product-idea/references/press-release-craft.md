# The Craft of Ivy Lee & Edward Bernays — Writing Voice Reference

Two men invented modern public relations from opposite poles. **Ivy Lee** gave PR its
*form* and *ethics* — the factual, journalist-ready press release. **Edward Bernays**
gave it its *psychology* and *narrative power* — the art of attaching a thing to a
desire. An elite press-release writer holds both in tension: Lee's credible spine,
Bernays's resonant frame.

Use Bernays to **choose the frame and angle** (which true facts lead, what larger human
stake the news serves). Use Lee to **build the body** (accurate facts, plain language,
inverted pyramid, verifiability). Frame the truth; never fabricate to fit the frame.

## 1. Ivy Lee (1877–1934) — father of the modern press release

### The Declaration of Principles (1906)
Issued to newspaper editors during a coal strike; the founding ethical charter of PR.
Verbatim core:

> "This is not a secret press bureau. All our work is done in the open. We aim to supply
> news. This is not an advertising agency... Our matter is accurate. Further details on
> any subject treated will be supplied promptly, and any editor will be assisted most
> cheerfully in verifying directly any statement of fact."

It encodes four still-canonical values: **openness** (no disguised advertising),
**accuracy** (backed by an offer to help editors verify), **honesty/disclosure** (name
who you represent), and **"the public be informed."** Lee's most-quoted maxim — *"Tell
the truth, because sooner or later the public will find out anyway"* — is widely
attributed but possibly apocryphal; the logic is sound regardless: accuracy is the only
durable source of credibility.

### The 1906 Atlantic City train wreck — the "first modern press release"
When a train ran off a drawbridge killing ~50 people, railroads of the era stonewalled.
Lee did the opposite: he got the railroad to volunteer its own factual account before
the press could assemble a hostile one, brought reporters to the site to verify
firsthand, issued a written statement the *New York Times* printed, and **reasoned
rather than merely asserted** (explaining why a mechanism defect was unlikely). It proved
transparency could be a reputational *asset*. (Note: only fragments of the original
wording survive; treat the wording as reconstructed-from-description.)

### Lee's craft principles
- **Inverted pyramid** — most important facts first; the lede carries the news.
- **Clarity** — plain declarative sentences a reporter can lift verbatim.
- **Brevity** — supply the news, nothing padded.
- **Factual tone** — let facts persuade; minimal promotional language.
- **Ready-to-print** — write it as the journalist would write it.
- **Verifiability** — offer to help confirm every statement of fact.
- **Accuracy as the root of credibility** — one lie destroys the franchise.
- **Get ahead of the story** — in crisis, disclose first and frame the facts before others do.

### How Lee structures a release
1. Lede = the news (the single most newsworthy fact).
2. Who / what / when / where / why in the first paragraph or two.
3. Facts before interpretation.
4. Supporting detail in descending order of importance (cut from the bottom, lose nothing).
5. Attribution and a path to verification.

### Lee — do's and don'ts
**Do:** lead with the news; state verifiable facts; disclose who you represent; write so
it prints unedited; in crisis, disclose first; keep it short and plain.
**Don't:** bury the lede under interpretation; exaggerate or spin; disguise advertising as
news; stonewall; assert without reasoning; make a claim you can't help someone verify.

## 2. Edward Bernays (1891–1995) — theorist of the "engineering of consent"

Freud's nephew; fused crowd psychology with psychoanalysis. People are driven less by
rational evaluation than by unconscious desire, herd instinct, and emotion — so
persuasion works by reaching those deeper motives, not by reciting features. His
operative leverage principle: *"If you can influence the leaders... you automatically
influence the group they sway."*

### The campaigns and the lesson each teaches
- **Torches of Freedom (1929)** — to break the taboo on women smoking in public, he
  framed cigarettes as symbols of emancipation and staged suffrage-aligned women lighting
  up in the Easter parade. → **Tie the product to a larger cultural aspiration.** Don't
  sell the cigarette; sell *freedom*. Make a product feel like a cause.
- **Bacon-and-eggs breakfast (1920s)** — had a physician survey ~5,000 doctors on whether
  a hearty breakfast was healthier; "4,500 physicians urge heavy breakfast" ran in
  headlines. → **Use third-party authority and social proof.** A neutral expert persuades
  where a brand's self-claim cannot.
- **Ivory soap sculpture contests** — turned soap into a national kids' carving
  competition embraced by schools. → **Make the audience feel the product is theirs.**
  Give people an activity, a ritual, a stage; participation persuades.

### Bernays's craft principles
- **Narrative framing** — meaning is constructed; choose the frame that recasts the product.
- **Tie product to a bigger idea/value/identity** — freedom, health, status, belonging, progress.
- **Appeal to deep motives**, not features — security, status, belonging, ease, self-image.
- **Third-party authority & social proof** — experts, customers, institutions, data carry the claim.
- **Influence the influencers** — move the leaders and the group follows.
- **Create felt need & cultural relevance** — manufacture the occasion and the conversation.
- **Make it feel organic** — participation beats overt advertising.

### Bernays — do's and don'ts
**Do:** find the underlying desire and frame to it; attach the offering to a larger
value/identity; recruit credible third parties; give the audience a role; lead with
meaning, not specs.
**Don't:** recite features; rely on the brand's own voice for credibility; ignore the
emotional/symbolic layer; confuse information with persuasion.
*(Ethical caution: his "without their knowing about it" and concealed sponsorship are
where his method crosses from persuasion into manipulation — see §4.)*

## 3. The fused elite voice — 12 actionable principles

Lee gives the **credible spine**; Bernays gives the **resonant frame**. Alone, Lee is
true but forgettable; Bernays is magnetic but untrustworthy. Fused: *a verifiable fact
delivered inside a frame that makes the reader feel why it matters to them.*

1. **Lead with the news, framed for meaning.** Inverted-pyramid lede, but choose the
   angle that connects to a larger human stake. The first sentence states what happened
   *and* signals why it matters.
2. **Anchor every claim in a verifiable fact.** Emotion rides on accuracy. If you can't
   help a journalist verify it, cut it.
3. **Attach the announcement to a bigger idea** — a value, identity, or cultural moment —
   without overstating what's true.
4. **Recruit credible third parties.** Quote experts, customers, data; social proof
   persuades where self-praise can't — and disclose who's speaking.
5. **Write it ready-to-print.** Plain, declarative, quotable sentences.
6. **Facts before interpretation; frame around both.** The frame guides selection of
   which true facts lead — never invention.
7. **Keep it brief; supply news, not adjectives.** One strong frame plus hard facts beats
   a pile of superlatives.
8. **Speak to a motive, not a feature.** Translate specs into what the reader gains —
   status, security, ease, identity.
9. **Give the audience a role.** Frame so the reader feels the news is *theirs* — a
   movement to join, a ritual, a stake.
10. **In crisis, disclose first and reason openly.** Transparency is a reputational asset.
11. **Provide attribution and a path to verification.** This is what makes a
    Bernays-style frame trustworthy rather than slick.
12. **Frame the truth — never manufacture it.** The synthesis only holds while the facts
    are real. The frame chooses emphasis; it never invents.

## 4. The ethical line

This is where the two men diverge most sharply, and it matters for every PR/FAQ. **Lee
draws the boundary: "All our work is done in the open... Our matter is accurate."**
Disclosure and verifiability. **Bernays repeatedly crossed it** — concealed sponsorship,
and the explicit aim to move people "without their knowing about it." That is
manipulation, not persuasion.

The defensible fusion takes **Bernays's psychological insight and narrative power,
governed by Lee's transparency and accuracy:**
- Frame to genuine human motives — but frame *true* facts.
- Use third-party authority — but disclose relationships rather than disguise them.
- Engineer attention and resonance — but never engineer belief in something false.

*Persuasion grounded in truth amplifies; manipulation grounded in concealment eventually
collapses* — which is exactly Lee's point that "sooner or later the public will find
out." The elite voice is Bernays's frame on Lee's foundation: emotionally resonant,
factually unimpeachable, openly sourced.
